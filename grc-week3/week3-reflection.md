## 1. Weekly Summary

This week had marked quite an important shift in my learning because I had moved beyond the technical cybersecurity tasks and started to explore the Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC). Earlier in the internship, I had mainly focused on SIEM, log analysis, and even identifying the security events. This week has helped me understand how those technical activities connect to business decisions and compliance requirements, as well as the risk management.

Throughout the week, I had learned how the security frameworks such as NIST 800-53 and ISO/IEC 27001 were able to provide a structured guidance for protecting the systems and information. I had then mapped common security findings to specific controls, as well as created a qualitative risk register using a likelihood × impact approach, and then finally prepared an executive compliance report that had summarized the organization's overall risk posture for non-technical leadership.

## 2. Technical Growth

This week has helped me understand how the GRC is able to connect to the SIEM and log-analysis work that I had completed earlier during the internship. Previously, my main focus had been on identifying the suspicious events and reviewing the logs, as well as understanding how the attacks could be detected. This week has shown me that those same technical activities were also able to support governance, compliance, and risk management.

Looking at the compliance side had changed the way I used to think about detections. For example, the security logs are not only useful for identifying incidents, but they are also able to demonstrate that important security controls are operating as expected. If the logging is missing or not even reviewed regularly, it tends to become both a technical weakness and a compliance gap.

The most challenging part for me this week has been translating the technical findings into a language that the non-technical leadership could easily understand. I had found that writing an executive summary required a different way of thinking because the focus was no longer on the technical details, but rather instead on the business impact, risk, as well as the practical recommendations that the management could act upon.

## 3. Applied Understanding

Out of the five security findings that I had worked with this week, I had found the weak authentication finding to be the most interesting to map to a security control. Before this exercise, I had mainly viewed it as a technical issue that could allow the unauthorized users to access an account. After mapping it to the relevant NIST 800-53 controls and including it in the risk register, I had understood that it also represented a compliance gap that could affect the organization's overall security posture.

This exercise has taught me that technical findings do not exist on their own. Every vulnerability can usually be linked to one or more security controls that are either missing or not fully implemented. By identifying those control gaps, the organizations are able to better prioritize the remediation efforts and reduce risk, as well as demonstrate compliance with recognized security frameworks.

## 4. Self-Assessment

Overall, I would rate my understanding of this week's topics as **9/10**. I believe I was able to develop a much better understanding of how governance, risk management, and compliance are able to work together within an organization. Completing the control mapping and risk register, along with the executive report, has also helped me understand how these technical findings can be translated into information that the management is able to use when making security decisions.

I am also satisfied with the quality of the work that I was able to produce this week because each assignment was built upon the previous one and maintained a consistent flow from identifying controls through to communicating the business risk. If there is one area that I would like to improve, it would be becoming even more confident when explaining technical cybersecurity concepts to non-technical audiences in a much simpler and more concise manner.
